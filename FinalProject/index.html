Opened in 1999,Semakau Landfill is Singapore's first and only offshore landfill located about 8 kilometers south of the main island of Singapore. The landfill covers an area of about 350 hectares and was created by building a seawall around two small islands, Pulau Sakeng and Pulau Semakau, and filling the area with waste. The landfill is equipped with advanced systems to manage leachate and landfill gas to minimize its environmental impact. Despite being a landfill, Semakau Landfill is also home to a diverse range of wildlife and is an important site for birdwatching and conservation efforts in Singapore.

Over the past 40 years, waste disposed in Singapore has increased seven-fold. In recent years, it receives more than 2,000 tonnes of WTE incineration ash and non- incinerable waste daily.

Based on current rate of waste disposal, Semakau Landfill is projected to reach its maximum capacity by 2035. A new offshore landfill will then be needed every 30 to 35 years. This is unsustainable in land scarce Singapore.

During the COVID-19 pandemic period, there was a decrease in the total amount of waste generated in Singapore due to lower construction activity and reduced export demands. However, despite this decrease, the amount of waste disposed almost remained the same due to a significant drop in the recycling rate.

The recycling rates, both for domestic and non-domestic waste, declined during the pandemic, possibly due to a pause in the collection of recyclable materials. Though the industrial sector has returned to normal, the domestic recycling rate remained stagnant at 13% in 2021 - the lowest in a decade.

To extend Semakau Landfill’s lifespan beyond 2035, the Zero Waste Masterplan has set an ambitious waste reduction target for Singapore – to reduce the waste sent to Semakau Landfill by 30 per cent by 2030. In this bubble diagram, the waste disposed is represented on the X-axis and waste generated on the Y-axis, with the size of the bubble indicating the disposal rate. The higher up and to the left a bubble is, the more concerning the waste stream. By examining the diagram, we can identify the three major waste sources: plastic, paper, and food waste.

When looking at recycling rates alone, textile, plastic, and glass have the highest disposal rates, which are difficult and not commercially viable to recycle. As responsible citizens and consumers, we can make a difference by reducing our consumption and practicing the 4Rs - Recycle, Reduce, Reuse, and Repair. Simple daily habits such as using fewer straws, bringing our own food containers, recycling properly, and bringing our own bags can make a significant impact.
